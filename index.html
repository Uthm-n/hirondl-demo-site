Obstacles we overcame
Raw material costs had risen sharply but prices had not kept pace. High variation in profitability across comparable accounts. Cost leakages from freight, returns, and payment terms invisible in reported margins.
Approach
Built rep-level price opportunity capture sheets. Set rep-specific improvement targets. Wave-based rollout with individual coaching. Power BI impact tracking dashboard. Revised distribution price book.
Impact
2 to 3 percentage point gross margin improvement sustained after engagement close. Sales teams began using the tool voluntarily in client meetings. Resistant markets became the strongest advocates.
Results visualised
Gross margin recovery across four engagement waves
Each wave represents approximately three months of implementation, starting with highest-alignment markets and using early results to bring resistant markets along.
The situation
Three years of transaction data showed the same product being sold to comparable customers at prices varying by up to thirty-five percent. The variation was structural, consistent across geographies, and entirely the result of autonomous rep decisions made without any shared pricing framework.

The recoverable opportunity was larger than the original investment thesis assumed. But recovering it required changing how the entire sales organisation thought about pricing, not just correcting a spreadsheet.

How we adapted mid-program
Initial group workshops met significant resistance in two markets. We shifted to individual coaching sessions working through each rep's own account data. Personal data proved far harder to dismiss than aggregate patterns. The wave-based rollout let early adopter markets serve as proof of concept for resistant ones.
Custom Tooling Built
Tools that stayed in use after we left
  • Rep-level price opportunity capture sheets, personalised per sales territory
  • Power BI gross margin tracking dashboard with wave-by-wave progress view
  • Revised distribution price book with floor pricing and discount logic
Obstacles we overcame
What we ran into and how we solved it
  • Strong rep resistance in two markets initially, with group workshops dismissed as "head office theory"
  • Shifted to 1:1 coaching using each rep's own account data; personal numbers proved harder to argue with
  • Early-adopter markets used as internal proof; resistant teams came along once peers showed results
AI Use Case
How AI accelerated this engagement
  • AI-assisted transaction clustering identified structural discount patterns across 3 years of data in days rather than 8 weeks of manual analysis
  • Automated flagging of accounts with the highest recoverable margin surfaced the 20% of accounts driving 80% of the opportunity
  • Enabled rep-specific insight packs to be built at scale, one per territory, without adding analyst headcount

Obstacles we overcame
Some market centres had no segmentation model. Others used sales-volume-based segmentation that rewarded historic activity rather than future potential. Reps spent time where relationships were comfortable, not where opportunity was largest.
Approach
Built a potential and SOW segmentation framework. Developed account plans and visit targets per rep per client. Identified high-potential low-SOW regions. Defined 10 to 20 must-win accounts per market centre.
Impact
Visits to low potential accounts fell 50 percent. Visits to high potential accounts rose 20 to 30 percent. SOW improved 3 to 5 percent per market. A new hunting capacity of 25 percent of rep time was unlocked for the first time.
Results visualised
Visit allocation before and after segmentation
Representative visits per week by customer tier. Effort was reallocated from low-potential accounts to high-potential and new hunting activity.
The situation
Sales reps were working hard. The problem was not effort; it was direction. When we mapped where time was actually being spent against where potential was highest, the mismatch was significant. Accounts receiving the most attention were ones reps had strong relationships with, not the ones with the largest untapped share of wallet.

The most common objection was that reps would lose C customer relationships. In practice, A customers, previously under-served, benefited most from the reallocation, and SOW gains followed within the first two quarters.

Custom Tooling Built
Tools that stayed in use after we left
  • Potential and share-of-wallet scoring model for 10,000+ accounts across 6 markets
  • Territory rebalancing tool allowing managers to redistribute accounts between reps based on potential
  • Segmentation heatmap by market centre, showing A/B/C split and visit allocation before and after
Obstacles we overcame
What we ran into and how we solved it
  • CRM data gaps in several markets meant potential scoring required external data enrichment before the model could run
  • Rep scepticism about losing C-account relationships, resolved through workshops showing individual account uplift data
  • Regional manager alignment took longer than expected; phased rollout gave each market time to calibrate
AI Use Case
How AI accelerated this engagement
  • ML-based potential scoring model processed 10,000+ customer accounts across 6 markets in hours, not weeks
  • Automated external data enrichment filled CRM gaps using company size, industry code, and web signals
  • AI-generated rep performance summaries surfaced outliers for manager review ahead of each coaching session

Obstacles we overcame
As-is GTM strategy not in line with growth ambition in Asia. No rigorous view of potential by market. Insufficient resource available in priority markets. Existing distributor network built on relationships, not strategy.
Approach
Bottom-up potential mapping for every Asian market. Market clustering into three strategic archetypes. Tailored GTM recommendation per cluster. Resource plan and subsidiary launch support for priority markets.
Impact
Two new subsidiaries opened within eighteen months. Double-digit regional sales growth achieved and sustained. Commercial team left with a repeatable framework for assessing new markets independently.
Results visualised
Market potential vs resource coverage — before and after
Illustrative view across 50 assessed markets, grouped by GTM archetype. The misalignment between potential and coverage was the core finding that drove the new strategy.
The situation
The business had operated across Asia for years through a distributor network that had grown incrementally. Each relationship had made sense in isolation. Collectively, they had created a commercial structure that bore almost no relationship to where the actual market opportunity sat.

The most common mistake in Asian market strategy is optimising for the markets you understand rather than the markets where the real potential sits. Getting to the right answer requires separating familiarity from opportunity.

How we adapted mid-engagement
Our initial brief was to recommend a single updated go-to-market model for the region. As the potential data came in, it became clear that a single model was the wrong answer. We shifted to a clustering methodology. Markets were grouped into archetypes based on potential, distributor reliability, competitive dynamics, and barriers to direct entry. Each archetype received a distinct GTM recommendation.
Custom Tooling Built
Tools that stayed in use after we left
  • Market entry scoring model ranking 50 Asian markets by potential, competitive intensity, and barrier to entry
  • Distributor evaluation framework with weighted scorecard used to rationalise and select partners per archetype
  • Year 1 P&L template for subsidiary launches, pre-built for two new markets
Obstacles we overcame
What we ran into and how we solved it
  • Limited reliable data in Tier 2 and Tier 3 markets, requiring triangulation from trade associations, field interviews, and public filings
  • Regulatory variation across 50 markets meant standard GTM assumptions had to be tested country-by-country
  • Incumbent distributors resistant to renegotiation, with a legal review and phased transition plan built into the programme

When the margin wasn't where the model said it was
By Cécile George · Hirondl · Published 2025
Context
The welding supply industry is built on volume, relationships, and tight margins. When a private equity firm acquires a business in this space, the value creation thesis almost always includes a pricing improvement component. Not because the business is badly managed, but because pricing discipline tends to erode quietly over time, one discount at a time, until the gap between what the business could charge and what it actually charges becomes structural rather than tactical.
The problem
When we pulled three years of transaction history, we found something more significant than a pricing gap. For the same product, sold to comparable customers, prices varied by up to thirty-five percent. The variation had nothing to do with volume, strategic value, or competitive pressure. It was the result of individual sales reps making discount decisions autonomously, without shared guidelines, over many years.
Then we built the pocket margin waterfall. Freight costs were being silently absorbed on low-volume routes. Extended payment terms were being offered as relationship courtesies with no adjustment to the effective price. Return credits were sitting against accounts that looked profitable on gross revenue alone.

The recoverable opportunity was larger than the original investment thesis had assumed. But recovering it required changing how the entire sales organisation thought about pricing, not just correcting a spreadsheet.

The solution
We built each sales rep a price opportunity capture tool tailored to their own book of business. Each rep could see their own discount patterns, the suggested target price, and the revenue impact of correcting specific accounts. We ran the program across four waves over twelve months, starting in markets where alignment was strongest and using those results to bring resistant markets along progressively.
The outcome was a two to three percentage point improvement in gross margin depending on the market, sustained after the engagement closed. The more meaningful signal was behavioural. The teams that had been most resistant at the start became the most vocal advocates for the tool.
What this means
A pricing intervention changes what people charge today. A pricing transformation changes how a commercial organisation thinks about pricing permanently. The gap between those two outcomes is almost never a data problem. It is a capability and change management problem.
